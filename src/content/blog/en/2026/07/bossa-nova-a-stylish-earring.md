## 1. Why wasn't it popular among the Black community?

Bossa Nova was born in the late 1950s in the social spots (apartments and clubs) of areas where the middle-to-upper class (mainly white people) lived, like Copacabana in Rio de Janeiro-nyo.

Here is what the structure of Brazilian society and the way music was received looked like back then-dajo.

* **Backlash against the "Bleaching" of Samba**
Originally, Samba was a blood-pumping music born from the physical cries and the rough percussive grooves hammered out by the Black people living in the slums (favelas). Bossa Nova stripped away the volume of the percussion instruments from Samba to the absolute limit, transforming it into a cool, quiet guitar strum (*batida*). To the Black community, this looked like, "You've turned the music of our souls into something spineless," -nyo.
* **The Gap in the Lyrical Worldview**
While the Black people's Samba sang earthily about "daily poverty," "the hardships of life," and "the joy of living," the lyrics of early Bossa Nova (like those by Vinícius de Moraes) were all about the carefree, romantic daily lives of the wealthy: "the sea, the sun, love, your eyes." From the perspective of the Black working class struggling to make ends meet, it was "carefree music from another world," -nyo.

## 2. It was actually slammed by the "White Intellectuals (Leftists)" too

Not just the Black community, but nationalist students and intellectuals in Brazil at the time also heavily criticized Bossa Nova, calling it "music that surrendered to the cultural invasion of America (Jazz)," -dajo.

Especially after the military coup in Brazil in 1964 brought about a dictatorship, the criticism grew even stronger: "Is this the time to be singing carefree songs about love and the sea?!" Because of this, some of the founding fathers of Bossa Nova like Carlos Lyra, as well as a young Chico Buarque and Caetano Veloso, steered towards music with stronger social messages (MPB: Música Popular Brasileira), -nyo.

## 3. A Massive Hit Abroad, and a "Reverse Import" Back Home

So why did it become so famous? Because **it was an explosive hit in America (and the world)**, -dajo.

The 1962 concert at Carnegie Hall, and the massive global hit of *Getz/Gilberto* (featuring "The Girl from Ipanema") in 1964. Because it was recognized by the world as "Brazil's cutting-edge, sophisticated culture," it gradually became accepted back home in Brazil as a "national pride" (transcending political conflicts), -nyo.
